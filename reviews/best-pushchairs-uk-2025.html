Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need a pushchair from day one?

Not necessarily. Many babies are happy in their parents' arms for the first few weeks. A pushchair becomes more useful around 4-6 weeks when babies start to be more alert and parents need hands-free time.

Save money by buying the pushchair you'll actually use for 1-4 years rather than an expensive "complete system" that becomes redundant after a few months.

What's the difference between a pushchair and a stroller?

Pushchairs are typically more robust with better suspension and all-terrain capability, while strollers are lighter and more compact. Pushchairs are better for daily use, while strollers are better for travel.

The crucial difference: pushchairs provide better comfort and durability, while strollers prioritize portability and convenience. Many parents find pushchairs more effective for daily use because of the better ride quality.

How important is ride quality really?

Extremely important for your actual use. Smooth ride (like Silver Cross) soothes babies effectively and feels natural. Bumpy or inconsistent ride can actually make babies more fussy and frustrate parents.

If you primarily use the pushchair for short periods, basic ride works fine. If you plan to use it frequently for daily walks, smooth ride prevents constant frustration and physical effort.

Can I use a pushchair for sleep?

No, pushchairs are not safe for sleep. Always follow the manufacturer's guidelines and current safety standards. Pushchairs are for supervised awake time only.

Look specifically for pushchairs that meet current safety standards and avoid any that claim to be suitable for sleep. This limitation affects enough parents that it deserves consideration before purchase.

Should I buy second-hand pushchairs?

Absolutely yes, with safety checks. Pushchairs typically see 1-4 years maximum use before children outgrow them, meaning "second-hand" often means "barely used."

Check the frame for cracks, test the wheels thoroughly, ensure the harness works perfectly, and verify all recline mechanisms function smoothly. Avoid pushchairs with worn-out parts or broken mechanisms, as replacements often cost more than buying another second-hand alternative. We exclusively bought second-hand after our first expensive pushchair, saving hundreds of pounds across multiple pushchairs.
