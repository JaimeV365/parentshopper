Frequently Asked Questions

How long can my baby stay in a bouncer?

Most manufacturers recommend limiting bouncer time to 20-30 minutes at a time, with a maximum of 2 hours per day. This prevents flat head syndrome and ensures proper development. Always supervise your baby in a bouncer.

When should I stop using a baby bouncer?

Stop using the bouncer when your baby can sit up unassisted (usually around 6 months) or reaches the weight limit, whichever comes first. Signs your baby has outgrown it include attempting to climb out or lean forward excessively.

Are baby bouncers safe for newborns?

Yes, when used correctly. Ensure the bouncer has proper head and neck support for newborns, never leave baby unattended, and always use the safety harness. Avoid bouncers with excessive recline that could restrict breathing.

Can baby bouncers help with reflux?

The slight incline of most bouncers can help with reflux by keeping baby upright after feeding. The Maxi-Cosi Kori's stationary mode is particularly good for this. However, always consult your GP for persistent reflux issues.

Should I get a bouncer or a swing?

Bouncers are more portable, take up less space, and many babies prefer the responsive bouncing motion. Swings offer hands-free soothing but are bulkier and more expensive. Many parents find bouncers more versatile for UK homes.

Should I buy a baby bouncer second-hand?

Bouncers are something parents can easily find second hand, but quite frankly, they will have been used by human beings at an age where they keep releasing all sorts of fluids, and leaving all sorts of traces and spots in this kind of product. So please check carefully before buying second hand. Beyond hygiene concerns, watch for worn straps, weakened springs, or missing parts. If you do go second-hand, the BabyBj√∂rn Bouncer Bliss is your safest bet due to its simple mechanism and fully removable fabric.
